Items and Equipment

Encumbrance

Exactly what gear a soldier has equipped, where it is equipped, and how quickly he could reach it, were crucial to success on the battlefield. A dagger in your backpack does no good when you drop your sword in a melee. Supply limitations were often decisive in warfare.

To capture this, Brigandine recommends a slot-based system of encumbrance that is carefully tracked.

Weight is not tracked in Brigandine. If an item is especially heavy, this should be represented by occupying multiple inventory slots, even if the item itself is physically small.

All gear is in one of three categories: Equipped, At-Hand, and Packed. What can be put where is limited by a number of slots in each category, where each slot can hold at most 1 item. Some larger items require more than 1 slot.

Equipped gear is held in either Dexter (right hand), or Sinister (left hand). There can be at most one item in each hand. Some items require both hands.

At-Hand gear can be quickly reached, either across the back or around the waist. This includes sidearms, packs, quivers, shields, belts, purses, or anything else. These items can be reached during combat by sacrificing all movement.

Packed items are stored within a container item, such as a backpack, purse, or side satchel. Retrieving them requires a lot of looking and fumbling, requiring an entire combat turn to be equipped. Additional turns are needed for each layer of recursion (e.g. an item inside a sack inside of a backpack).

Depending on your preference, there are two ways for this.

Option 1: Graphical Slots

All slots (Dexter/Sinister/Back/Waist) are represented graphically, as areas occupying a certain height and width of square grid spaces on a sheet of graph paper. Likewise, each item is represented by a block cut out of graph paper, to a certain size and shape. Items may be placed into any slot where hey fit. A slot with any item in it, at all, however small, occupies the entire slot. Items which are too large for a single slot may be fit over multiple slots. For instance, a two-handed sword, which is 4x4 slots, can be spread over Dexter and Sinister slots when equipped.

Equiped At-Hand

Each iventory slot is bound by thick black lines, while the thinner gray lines indicate spaces on the graph paper.

A carried container, such as a backpack, adds additional spaces where items may be stored. There is no limit on bag-within-bag recursion, as that is automatically handled by bags themselves taking up a lot of space in the inventory.

Option 2: Simplified Slots

Use simple slots, and allow the players to write items in the spaces below. One the lines are filled up, there is no more room to store anything. A backpack, pouch, purse, satchel, or other container can add more slots. Recursion of bags-within-bags is limited to 1 bag within another bag.

Arms and Armor

Arms

Medieval combatants carried several arms. A pole arm for charges, a sword for up-close battle, a warhammer for armored foes, a dagger for grappling. They carried these because some weapons were efficient, others weren't, depending on how the enemy was armored.

In other RPGs, the choice of battle axe, two-handed sword, or warhammer (which is really a maul...) is almost entirely cosmetic; they all hit the same and do basically the same damage. In Brigandine, the strategic choice of arms is a crucial part of the gameplay. Players must carefully gear to have equipped and at-hand to deal with whatever foes they come against.

All arms will have the following propertes:

weapon class
broad classifications for weapons: sword, axe, pole arm, etc.
weapon type
the specific type of weapon: a longsword, a morning star, etc.
weapon rank
a numerical value relating how the length or speed of wthe weapon.

List and description of arms

Swords

Pole arms

These weapons all feature a bladed or pointed implement attached to the end of a wooden shaft. The primary goal of these weapons is reach, hitting the target before the target can close.

spear
The most widely used weapon, from pre-history into the middle ages. It has been so widely used because of its versatility. It can be used one-handed with a shield, two-handed as a pole arm, and set against cavalry charges.
sill hook/guisarme/halberd
These three classifications, plus any others, are effectively the same due to a kind of covergenet evolution of arms. They began as simple farming tools stick to the end of a long stick, and developed in response to battlefield needs. All three feature a blade for cutting, a front spike for thrusting, a back spike for catching or tripping, set on a haft of about 8-12 ft. The primary difference in use will be tied to nation and class. The English prefer bill hooks, those on the Continent prefer guisarms, and richer warriors will prefer a halberd.
pike
These are long spears over 15 ft in length, which are particularly effective when used in formation againat cavalry. A pikeman in man-to-man combat is almost guaranteed the first strike before the opponent can close. Once the opponent closes, the pikeman should consider swapping to a side arm.
Missile weapons
Any weapon which hits from a distance. A character using missile weapons does not roll on the usual combat tables, and instead on the missile fire tables.
self bow
The simplest kind of bow, made from a single piece of wood. These are required to be long bows, and so cannot be used ahorse.
composite bow
Made by layers wood, sinew, and horn which have been laminated together with resin. Because they can achieve the same draw weight with less length, they can be used from horseback.
English longbow
The English are renowned for their tradition of longbow. These bows have very heavy draw weights, and requires constant training to handle. Not any archer can use an English longbow.
crossbow
sling
A common weapon of shepherds, used for throwing stones at speeds lethal to lions, bears, or even giants.
plumbatae
Also known as wardarts. Short throwing spears with leaden weights to help the sharp barb punch into armor. Not for use in public houses.

Armor

Medieval combatants took time to strap many peices of weighty metal plating all over their body. Metal gloves, metal greaves, metal shoes, metal skirting, metal breastplates, metal arm covers, all of which needed to be strapped in place by someone assisting. They did this because, covered in armored plating, the knight became nearly impervious to all but specialized weapons or incredibly well-aimed blows.

Armor class in Brigandine is literally the classification of the armor. The armor classes are as follows:

textile
including gambeson, padded jack, and boiled leather.
mail
including chainmail, scale armor, splint armor, and brigandine.
plate
including hardened steel cuirass, greaves, vanbraces, guantlets.
suit-of-plate
precisely interlocking metal plate armor, covering head to toe.

Note the difference between plate (hardened steel strapped to the body) and suit-of-plate. Suit-of-plate has been specially tailored to an individual. This requires a professional armorer and his apprentices working for several weeks. Suit-of-plate is therefore prohibitively expensive. Even if found in the course of an adventure, it needs to be fit by an armorer, or else it only counts as mail for armor purposes (the awkward fit makes armor joints stick or refuse to bend properly, so that it is less effective than plate).

Any of these armors can be combined with a shield, which will make the defender less likely to suffer a deadly blow. If the defender is already wearing suit-of-plate, then a shield adds no further benefit.

List and description of armors

The numbers needed to hit armor should not be the only things taken into account with armor. Consider the following properties of each armor type, and work them into any combat that occurs.

Gambeson
Armor made from thickly packed flax sewn into a linen tunic. This thick padding dampens blows from bludgeoning weapons. Bladed weapons will be stopped, but can still cut into the fabric, possibly even drawing blood. Piercing weapons such as arrows or spears can be driven through the layers if driven hard enough. Gambeson is great for keeping warm in cold weather, but miserable to wear in hot climates. If soaked in water, it provides great defense against fire. If not soaked in water, it is flammabe. Gambeson is flexible and quiet. It does not restrict arm rotation, nor prevent sneaking.
Leather
Made of the thick tanned hide of an animal, then shaped by boiling. It is very tough, and can not be easily cut or pierced. The armor absorbs some damage from bludgeoning attacks, but not very much. Leather has similar insulatory properties to gambeson. It will be warm in the winter, but not too hot in warm climates. It provides great protection from extreme heat, and is resistive to catching fire. Leather armor is flexible and quiet. It does not prevent thieves from sneaking or crawling around.
Chainmail
More properly, just "mail." Thousands of tiny rings of steel nitted together into a net that fits as a tunic. Effectively stops slashing attacks from bladed weapons, but vulnerable to piercing weapons such as arrows landing in the gaps of the tiny rings. Chainmail is flexible and does not restrict arm rotation, but is too noisy to wear while sneaking.
Scale armor
Similar in relevant ways to mail, but made of hundreds of tiny plates of metal banded together. Still vulnerable to piercing attacks between the banded plates. Scale armor is not as flexible as mail, and is too noisy to wear while sneaking.
Splint armor
Similar in principle to scale armor. Made of many long, thin bands of metal fastened together or sewn into a garment. The metal bands will flex with the wearer's motion. Similar vulnerabilities as scale armor and mail. It is not as flexible as mail, and is too noisy to wear while sneaking.
Brigandine
Similar to splint armor. A leather tunic. On the inside, many overlapping bands of metal have been riveted together into the tunic. The rivets seen form the exterior look like small studs, so that this armor is sometimes mistakenly called "studded leather." Provides full range of arm rotation. The leather exteior damps the sound of the metal plates, so that it is quiet enough to wear while sneaking
Plate
Hardened steel, shaped to fit the human body. Has to be strapped into place, which is a time-consuming process and may require assistance. This category includes any manner of cuirass or breast plate, in combination with helmet, metal greaves (leg protection), vanbraces (upper arm protection), and gauntlets (metal articulated gloves). Any area not covered directly by plate is assumed to have either mail or gambeson protection underneath.
