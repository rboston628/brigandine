Combat

In Brigandine, there is not merely one kind of combat, but four. They are as follows:

Skirmish
Used for quick resolutions, or for combat against weaker, unimportant foes.
Man-to-Man
Used to simulate in detail acts of swordplay and combat.
Jousting
A specialized system for choosing placement of strikes for single-combat duels.
Fantasy Combat
For use against foes which can otherwise not be slain by mortal men.

Any of these four may appear in the course of an adventure.

In general, all combat involves determining the attack and defensive strengths of each side (e.g. attk 2HF, def 1LC), determining the number of strikes each side may make, rolling the requisite dice (almost always ATK with melee and AIM with missile fire), then resolving successful strikes to determine victory.

Each system is explained below.

Combat Order

Whenever players are within missile range of enemies and it is determined that combat shoud occur, all players will roll IQ to determine the order of initiative. Each player rolls, not each character. The referee may split up or combine the enemy forces in initiative however makes sense for managing play. Initiative moves from largest number, to smallest. If any tie for initiative occurs, those characters will act simultaneously.

Once combat begins, it proceeds as follows:

  1. All players state what they intend to do over the round: firing missiles, moving, melee, etc. Artillery fire is declared now, and cannot be changed after it's declared. Any player may elect now to delay initiative; treat that player's initiative as a "1". Any communication between characters occurs here. Any reaction rolls (surprise, morale, etc.) occur here.
  2. All combatants move up to half movement, in initiative order.
  3. All missile file (arrows/slings) is taken, in initiative order.
  4. Melees are resolved.
  5. Any artillery fire declared occurs now, in initiative order.
  6. Any figure which did not engage in melee may move up to half movement, in initiative order.
  7. Any archer who only engaged in missile fire (no movement, artillery, or melee) may fire a second time, in initiative order.

If the above becomes tedious, then consider this condensed combat order:

  1. All players state what they intend to do over the round: firing missiles, moving, melee, etc. Artillery fire is declared now, and cannot be changed after it's declared. Any player may elect now to delay initiative; treat that player's initiative as a "1". Any communication between characters occurs here. Any reaction rolls (surprise, morale, etc.) occur here.
  2. Each player acts in initiative order. Each PC may
    • move up to half movement then attack,
    • move up to full movement without an attack,
    • or fire a missle then up to full movement
    Any declared atillery fire occurs on the artillerist's turn in initiative.
  3. Any archer who only engaged in missile fire (no movement, artillery, or melee) may fire a second time, in initiative order.

Note that in both systems, all combatants resolve the first step, then all combatants resolve the second step, and so on. In each step, combatants act in initiative order.

The second firing round is for archers specifically. This does not include crossbowmen, artillery, arquebusiers, slingers, or any other shooter with a weapon other than a bow.

These steps (whichever chosen) repeat until the combat is over

It is advised to re-roll initiative at the start of each round, but this is up to preference.

Skirmish Combat

This is the default combat system of the game, and serves as a baseline for all other combat.

Skirmish combat derives from wargame rules for units of several figures fighting. Units of troops are broken into either foot (infantry) or horse (cavalry), and further classified as light, medium, or heavy:

Foot Cavalry
Light LF infantry fighting in loose formation with basic weapons and limited gear. LC mounted soldiers mostly used for harrying opponents.
Medium MF infantry in closer formation with better weapons and moderate armor. MC mounted soldiers for direct assaults on enemy troops.
Heavy HF elite infantry units, able to withstand a cavalry charge. HC armored knights and shock troops to destroy enemy lines.

In Brigandine, a unit representing a single soldier may have the fighting strength of multiple men. The resolution is as follows:

  1. Determine the attacker's type and strength:
    • attk type --- whether LF, MF, HF, LC, MC, HC
    • #attk --- representing the effective number of men fighting
  2. Determine the defender's type and strength:
    • def type --- whether LF, MF, HF, LC, MC, HC
    • #def --- representing the number of hits needed to kill defender
  3. Find cell in table corresponding to attacker/defender types. This cell lists the number of dice per man that the attacker will roll, followed by the necessary result for a success.
  4. Roll the total number of dice indicated, and count each success as a hit. The dice rolled are according to the ATK stat, unless otherwise specified.
  5. The defender may elect to sacrifice his attack by taking defensive action, rolling as many DEF dice as his #def. Each rolled DEF die can be matched to one of the attacker's rolled ATK die, and if greater will negate it.
  6. Subtract remaining ATK dice from the defender's #def.
  7. If the defender is reduced to 0 #def, then the defender has been defeated.
  8. Continue to next attacker.

Jousting

Coming Soon!

Jousting combat is a form of dueling, fought by a series of direct charges with aimed strikes and carefuly chosen defenses. Each combatant chooses a place to aim and a defensive stance, which will both determine the outcome when the warriors collide in combat. There is no dice rolling in jousting.

It is important the jousters make their decisions in secret before the joust. This can be as simple as the referee writing down the opponents choices and then the player announcing the PC's choices.

Jousting lasts until an opponent is slain or unhorsed. In a friendly competition, a score may be kept instead of casualties.
